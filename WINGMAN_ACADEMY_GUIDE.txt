  ACADEMY PHILOSOPHY
  ──────────────────
  Most trading courses teach theory. Wingman Academy teaches through doing.
  Every stage ends with a real sim trade challenge. The AI grades the trade,
  not a quiz. Knowledge is demonstrated by execution, not by correct answers.

  This means a student who reads every lesson but trades badly does not pass.
  A student who trades well demonstrates they have internalised the lesson.
  That is the difference between a certificate and a credential.

  STAGE STRUCTURE (all 7 stages follow this pattern):
    1. LEARN    — Lesson content (text, visuals, interactive examples)
    2. TOOLS    — Relevant tools introduced and practised on the Wingman chart
    3. CHALLENGE — Specific sim trade criteria that must be met to pass
    4. DEBRIEF  — AI feedback on the trade submitted for grading
    5. UNLOCK   — Next stage unlocks after debrief acknowledged

  PASS CONDITIONS (universal across all stages):
    - Student must complete the LEARN section (scroll to bottom)
    - Student must submit at least one qualifying sim trade
    - The sim trade must meet the stage-specific criteria
    - If the trade fails grading: 24-hour cooldown before retry
    - Three consecutive failures triggers an AI intervention session
      before any further attempts are allowed

STAGE 1 — THE LANGUAGE OF MARKETS
Subtitle: "Before you can trade, you must learn to read."
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  LEARN — LESSON CONTENT
  ───────────────────────

  1.1 What Is a Financial Market?
      A financial market is simply a place — physical or digital — where
      buyers and sellers agree on a price for an asset. When more people
      want to buy than sell, price rises. When more want to sell than buy,
      price falls. Everything in trading flows from this one truth.

      Markets you will encounter in Wingman:
      - Forex (foreign exchange): Currency pairs. EUR/USD means the price
        of one Euro in US Dollars. The forex market trades $7.5 trillion
        per day — the largest and most liquid market on Earth.
      - Commodities: Physical goods. Gold (XAUUSD), Silver (XAGUSD),
        Oil (USOIL). Prices move on supply, demand, and geopolitics.
      - Indices: Baskets of stocks. US30 (Dow Jones), SPX500 (S&P 500),
        NAS100 (Nasdaq). They represent the health of an economy.
      - Crypto: Digital assets. Bitcoin (BTCUSD), Ethereum (ETHUSD).
        Highly volatile, 24/7 trading, no central authority.

  1.2 Reading a Price: Bid, Ask, and Spread
      Every market has two prices at all times:
      - BID:  The price a buyer will pay (you sell at this price)
      - ASK:  The price a seller wants (you buy at this price)
      - SPREAD: The difference between them. This is the broker's fee.

      Example: EUR/USD Bid 1.08450 | Ask 1.08465
      Spread = 1.5 pips. If you buy, you immediately start 1.5 pips
      in the negative. The market must move 1.5 pips in your favour
      before you break even. This is why small spreads matter.

      Rule: Always check the spread before trading.
      Spreads widen during low liquidity (weekends, news events).

  1.3 Pips, Points, and Lot Sizes
      PIP (Price Interest Point):
        The smallest standard price movement.
        For EUR/USD: 0.0001 = 1 pip
        For USD/JPY: 0.01 = 1 pip
        For XAUUSD (Gold): 0.01 = 1 pip (often called a point)

      LOT SIZE — how much you are trading:
        Standard Lot:  100,000 units. 1 pip = $10 profit/loss
        Mini Lot:       10,000 units. 1 pip = $1 profit/loss
        Micro Lot:       1,000 units. 1 pip = $0.10 profit/loss
        Nano Lot:          100 units. 1 pip = $0.01 profit/loss

      In the Wingman Sim Trader, you always trade micro lots unless
      you specifically increase size. This protects your sim balance
      while you learn.

  1.4 Long vs Short
      GOING LONG = Buying. You believe price will rise.
        You buy EUR/USD at 1.0845. Price rises to 1.0865.
        You made 20 pips profit.

      GOING SHORT = Selling. You believe price will fall.
        You sell (short) XAUUSD at 2350.00. Price falls to 2340.00.
        You made 10 points profit.

      Critical insight: In forex and CFDs, you can profit from falling
      prices just as easily as rising prices. Direction does not matter.
      Being right about direction does.

  1.5 What Is a CFD?
      Contract for Difference. You do not own the underlying asset.
      You speculate on whether the price will rise or fall.
      Profits and losses are settled in cash.
      This is what Wingman Sim Trader uses. You never own the currency
      or commodity — you hold a position on its price movement.

  1.6 Market Hours and Sessions
      Unlike stocks, forex trades 24 hours a day, 5 days a week.
      It is divided into four main sessions:

      Sydney Session:    10pm – 7am UTC     (low volatility)
      Tokyo Session:     midnight – 9am UTC (JPY pairs active)
      London Session:    8am – 5pm UTC      (highest volume, EUR/GBP)
      New York Session:  1pm – 10pm UTC     (USD pairs active)
      London+NY Overlap: 1pm – 5pm UTC      ← GOLDEN WINDOW
        This 4-hour overlap has the highest liquidity and tightest
        spreads. Most professional traders focus here.

      Rule: Know which session you are in. Trading EURUSD at 3am UTC
      when Tokyo is the only active session means wide spreads and
      choppy, unpredictable moves.

  1.7 Reading a Candlestick Chart
      The candlestick is the most important visual tool in trading.
      Each candle represents one time period (1 minute, 1 hour, 1 day).

      A single candle contains four prices:
        OPEN:  Where price was at the start of the period
        HIGH:  The highest price reached during the period
        LOW:   The lowest price reached during the period
        CLOSE: Where price was at the end of the period

      BULLISH candle (green/white): Close > Open. Price rose.
      BEARISH candle (red/black):   Close < Open. Price fell.

      The BODY is the rectangle between open and close.
      The WICKS (shadows) are the thin lines above and below.

      What wicks tell you:
        Long upper wick: Price tried to go higher but was rejected.
                         Sellers pushed back.
        Long lower wick: Price tried to go lower but was rejected.
                         Buyers pushed back.
        Small wicks:     Strong directional move. Little resistance.

      Key patterns (memorise these — they appear in every stage):
        DOJI:         Open ≈ Close. Tiny body, long wicks. Indecision.
                      Often signals a reversal when it appears at a high
                      or low after a strong move.
        HAMMER:       Small body at top, long lower wick. Found at lows.
                      Buyers rejected a move down. Potential reversal up.
        SHOOTING STAR: Small body at bottom, long upper wick. Found at highs.
                      Sellers rejected a move up. Potential reversal down.
        ENGULFING:    One candle's body completely contains the previous
                      candle's body. Strong reversal signal.
                      Bullish engulfing (green swallows red) = buy signal.
                      Bearish engulfing (red swallows green) = sell signal.
        MARUBOZU:     No wicks. Pure body. Extreme directional conviction.

STAGE 2 — SUPPORT, RESISTANCE, AND STRUCTURE
Subtitle: "The market has a memory. Learn to read it."
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  LEARN — LESSON CONTENT
  ───────────────────────

  2.1 Why Price Respects Levels
      Markets are driven by human decisions. Humans remember prices.
      When EUR/USD bounced off 1.0800 three times in the last year,
      traders remember that level. They place orders there again.
      That collective memory creates what we call Support and Resistance.

      SUPPORT: A price level where buying pressure has historically
               been strong enough to stop price falling further.
               Think of it as a floor.

      RESISTANCE: A price level where selling pressure has historically
                  been strong enough to stop price rising further.
                  Think of it as a ceiling.

  2.2 How to Draw Support and Resistance
      Rules for valid S/R levels:
      - Price must have touched the level at least twice
      - The more touches, the stronger the level
      - Levels from higher timeframes (4H, Daily) are more significant
        than levels from lower timeframes (1M, 5M)
      - Round numbers (1.0800, 2000.00, 50000) often act as S/R
        because humans naturally cluster orders around them

      The process:
      1. Open the 1D or 4H chart
      2. Look for obvious turning points — places price clearly
         reversed direction
      3. Draw a horizontal line through the wicks of those turns
      4. Switch to the 1H chart and refine
      5. Note levels that appear on multiple timeframes — those are
         the most important

  2.3 Role Reversal
      One of the most powerful concepts in technical analysis:
      When price breaks through a resistance level and closes above it,
      that old resistance often becomes the new support.
      When price breaks through a support level and closes below it,
      that old support often becomes the new resistance.

      Why it happens: Traders who were "wrong" at the old level now
      want to exit at breakeven when price returns to that level.
      Their orders create the reversal that confirms role reversal.

  2.4 Trend Structure: Higher Highs and Lower Lows
      A trend is not just "price going up." A trend has structure.

      UPTREND:     Higher Highs (HH) + Higher Lows (HL)
                   Each rally exceeds the last. Each pullback holds
                   above the last pullback low.

      DOWNTREND:   Lower Highs (LH) + Lower Lows (LL)
                   Each sell-off goes lower. Each bounce fails to
                   reach the previous high.

      RANGING:     No clear HH/HL or LH/LL sequence.
                   Price oscillates between a support and resistance.
                   Many beginners lose money trading ranges as trends.

      The most dangerous mistake: entering a "trend" trade when
      the structure has already broken. Always check the last 3
      swing highs and lows before calling a direction.

  2.5 Zones vs Lines
      In practice, support and resistance are not precise lines.
      They are zones — areas where price tends to react.
      Drawing them as narrow rectangles (zones) rather than single
      lines gives you more realistic expectations.

      A price entering a zone should make you alert.
      A price closing clearly through a zone is a breakout signal.
      A price touching a zone and reversing is a bounce signal.

  2.6 Key Market Structures
      SWING HIGH: A candle with a lower high on both sides of it.
                  The highest point of a recent move.
      SWING LOW:  A candle with a higher low on both sides of it.
                  The lowest point of a recent move.

      BREAK OF STRUCTURE (BOS): Price closes beyond a previous swing
      high (bullish BOS) or swing low (bearish BOS). This is the
      earliest signal that a trend may be changing.

      CHANGE OF CHARACTER (CHoCH): After a series of HH/HL in an
      uptrend, price makes a lower low for the first time. This is
      a stronger signal — the trend may be reversing, not just
      pausing.

  2.7 Trading With the Trend
      "The trend is your friend until it bends."

      In an uptrend: Look to buy pullbacks to support or previous
      resistance that has flipped to support. Do not sell just
      because price looks "high."

      In a downtrend: Look to sell bounces to resistance or previous
      support that has flipped to resistance. Do not buy just because
      price looks "cheap."

      In a range: Buy the support zone, sell the resistance zone.
      Use tighter stops and smaller targets. Ranges break — and when
      they do, they move fast.

STAGE 3 — TECHNICAL INDICATORS
Subtitle: "Indicators don't predict. They describe. Know the difference."
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  LEARN — LESSON CONTENT
  ───────────────────────

  3.1 What Indicators Actually Are
      Every indicator is a mathematical formula applied to historical
      price data. They do not predict the future. They describe the
      past in a way that helps you assess the present.

      Common mistake: Using indicators as entry signals in isolation.
      Correct use: Using indicators to confirm what price structure
      and S/R levels already suggest.

      Indicators are divided into two categories:
      LAGGING (trend-following): Based on past data. Confirm trend.
        Examples: Moving Averages, MACD, Bollinger Bands
      LEADING (momentum): Try to anticipate reversals. Often oscillate.
        Examples: RSI, Stochastic, CCI

  3.2 Moving Averages (MA)
      The most widely used indicator in existence.
      A moving average smooths price by averaging the last N candles.

      SIMPLE MOVING AVERAGE (SMA):
        SMA(20) = sum of last 20 closing prices ÷ 20
        All 20 periods are weighted equally.
        Slower to react. Cleaner on higher timeframes.

      EXPONENTIAL MOVING AVERAGE (EMA):
        Gives more weight to recent prices.
        Faster to react to new price moves.
        EMA(9) and EMA(21) are popular for short-term traders.
        EMA(50) and EMA(200) are popular for trend identification.

      KEY USES:
        Trend direction:  Price above MA = uptrend bias
                          Price below MA = downtrend bias
        Dynamic S/R:      Price often bounces off the 20 EMA or 50 EMA
                          in trending markets
        MA crossover:     When the fast MA crosses above the slow MA,
                          bullish signal (Golden Cross at 50/200)
                          When fast crosses below slow,
                          bearish signal (Death Cross at 50/200)

      IMPORTANT LIMITATION: Moving averages lag. By the time the
      crossover happens, the move has often already started. Use them
      for confirmation, not prediction.

  3.3 RSI — Relative Strength Index
      Measures the speed and magnitude of recent price changes.
      Oscillates between 0 and 100.
      Default period: 14.

      Traditional interpretation:
        RSI above 70: Overbought (price may be due for a pullback)
        RSI below 30: Oversold (price may be due for a bounce)
        RSI at 50:    Neutral / transition zone

      PROFESSIONAL USE (this is what actually works):
        Overbought/oversold signals alone are not enough. Price can
        stay "overbought" for weeks in a strong trend.

        RSI DIVERGENCE is the real power:
          Regular Bullish Divergence:
            Price makes a lower low, RSI makes a higher low.
            Downside momentum is weakening. Potential reversal up.
          Regular Bearish Divergence:
            Price makes a higher high, RSI makes a lower high.
            Upside momentum is weakening. Potential reversal down.
          Hidden Bullish Divergence:
            Price makes a higher low, RSI makes a lower low.
            Trend continuation signal in an uptrend.

  3.4 MACD — Moving Average Convergence Divergence
      Consists of three components:
        MACD Line:   EMA(12) minus EMA(26)
        Signal Line: EMA(9) of the MACD Line
        Histogram:   MACD Line minus Signal Line (visualises distance)

      Default settings: 12, 26, 9

      KEY USES:
        Crossover signal:
          MACD Line crosses above Signal Line: bullish momentum
          MACD Line crosses below Signal Line: bearish momentum
        Zero line cross:
          MACD crossing above zero: uptrend confirmation
          MACD crossing below zero: downtrend confirmation
        Histogram shrinking: Momentum is fading. Possible reversal.
        Divergence: Same principle as RSI divergence. Very powerful
          when MACD histogram diverges from price.

  3.5 Bollinger Bands
      Consists of three lines:
        Middle Band:  20-period SMA
        Upper Band:   Middle + 2 standard deviations
        Lower Band:   Middle − 2 standard deviations

      Statistical principle: ~95% of price action falls within the bands.
      When price touches or exceeds a band, it is statistically unusual.

      KEY USES:
        SQUEEZE: When the bands narrow, volatility is low.
          A breakout often follows. Direction is unknown — wait for
          the candle close to confirm which way.
        WALK THE BAND: In a strong trend, price can "walk" along the
          upper or lower band for extended periods. Do not fade it.
        MEAN REVERSION: In ranging markets, price touching the upper
          band and returning to the middle is a reliable pattern.
          Same for the lower band.
        WIDTH: Expanding bands = increasing volatility.
               Contracting bands = decreasing volatility.

  3.6 Stochastic Oscillator
      Compares a closing price to its price range over N periods.
      Outputs two lines: %K (fast) and %D (slow).
      Range: 0 to 100.

      Traditional zones: above 80 = overbought, below 20 = oversold.
      The CROSSOVER of %K and %D within these zones is the signal:
        %K crosses above %D below 20: buy signal
        %K crosses below %D above 80: sell signal

      Stochastic works best in ranging/sideways markets.
      In strong trends, it gives false signals constantly.
      Always check the trend before using it.

  3.7 ATR — Average True Range
      Measures market volatility. Not a directional indicator.
      ATR tells you how much price TYPICALLY moves in one period.

      KEY USES:
        Stop Loss sizing:
          If XAUUSD ATR(14) on the 1H chart is 8 points,
          a stop of 8–12 points gives price room to breathe.
          A stop of 2 points will almost certainly be hit by noise.
        Position sizing:
          Wider ATR = smaller position size (more risk per pip)
          Narrow ATR = larger position size (less risk per pip)
        Trend assessment:
          Rising ATR = volatility increasing. Could be a breakout.
          Falling ATR = volatility decreasing. Could be a squeeze.

  3.8 Volume
      Volume represents the number of trades executed in a period.
      Higher volume = more conviction behind the move.
      Lower volume = less conviction. Move may not sustain.

      KEY RULES:
        Breakout on high volume: More likely to sustain.
        Breakout on low volume: More likely to fail (fake breakout).
        Price rising but volume falling: Trend weakening.
        Price falling but volume falling: Sellers losing conviction.

      Note: In forex, true volume data is not available
      (it's a decentralised market). Tick volume (number of price
      changes) is used as a proxy and is generally reliable.

  3.9 Combining Indicators: The Confluence Approach
      No single indicator is reliable alone. Professional traders
      look for CONFLUENCE — multiple independent signals agreeing.

      Example of a high-confluence long setup:
        1. Price is above the 50 EMA (trend filter: bullish)
        2. Price has pulled back to a key support zone
        3. RSI is at 35 and showing bullish divergence
        4. MACD histogram is starting to rise from negative territory
        5. A bullish engulfing candle forms at the support zone
        6. ATR shows normal volatility (not an extreme spike)

      Six independent signals all pointing the same way.
      That is a high-confidence trade. One signal alone is noise.

  3.10 What Indicators Cannot Do
       - They cannot predict the future
       - They cannot account for sudden news events
       - They cannot tell you position size
       - They lag behind the market (especially lagging indicators)
       - They are all derived from the same thing: price

       The trader who relies only on indicators will eventually be

STAGE 4 — RISK MANAGEMENT AND PSYCHOLOGY
Subtitle: "Every professional trader's actual edge is here."
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  LEARN — LESSON CONTENT

  4.1 Why Risk Management Is the Actual Edge
      Most beginners believe their edge comes from finding better entries.
      Professional traders know the real edge is in how you manage loss.

      Mathematical reality:
        A trader who wins 40% of trades with 1:3 R/R is profitable.
        A trader who wins 70% of trades with 1:0.5 R/R is not.

        40% win rate × 3R gain = 1.2R average
        60% loss rate × 1R loss = 0.6R average
        Net: +0.6R per trade. Profitable.

        70% win rate × 0.5R gain = 0.35R average
        30% loss rate × 1R loss = 0.3R average
        Net: +0.05R per trade. Barely profitable — and fragile.

      The implication: You do not need to be right most of the time.
      You need to lose small when wrong and win big when right.

  4.2 The 1% Rule
      Never risk more than 1–2% of your trading account on a single trade.

      Why: If you risk 1% per trade and have 10 consecutive losses
      (unlikely but possible), you have lost 10% of your account.
      That is recoverable. If you risk 10% per trade and lose 10 in a
      row, you have lost your entire account.

      How to calculate:
        Account: $10,000
        Risk per trade: 1% = $100
        Stop Loss: 20 pips on EUR/USD
        Pip value (micro lot): $0.10
        Position size: $100 ÷ (20 × $0.10) = 50 micro lots = 0.05 lots

      This calculation is built into the Wingman Risk Calculator.
      Students should use it on every trade — not guess the size.

  4.3 Risk/Reward Ratios Explained
      R/R = (Take Profit distance) ÷ (Stop Loss distance)

        R/R of 1:1 — For every $1 risked, you gain $1. 
                     You need >50% win rate to be profitable.
        R/R of 1:2 — For every $1 risked, you gain $2.
                     You need >33% win rate to be profitable.
        R/R of 1:3 — For every $1 risked, you gain $3.
                     You need >25% win rate to be profitable.

      Minimum acceptable R/R in Wingman Academy: 1:1.5
      Recommended for consistent profitability: 1:2 or above.

      Note: Higher R/R ratios are harder to achieve. A 1:5 R/R trade
      often means the target is so far away it is never reached.
      Find the balance between realistic targets and good ratios.

  4.4 Drawdown and Recovery
      DRAWDOWN: The decline from a peak in your account balance.
      Maximum Drawdown: The largest peak-to-trough decline ever.

      The brutal mathematics of recovery:
        10% loss requires 11% gain to recover
        20% loss requires 25% gain to recover
        30% loss requires 43% gain to recover
        50% loss requires 100% gain to recover
        80% loss requires 400% gain to recover

      This is why preserving capital is everything in trading.
      It is easier to not lose money than to make it back.

  4.5 The Six Trading Psychology Traps
      These are the Wingman Behaviour Detectors. Know them by name.

      1. REVENGE TRADING
         After a losing trade, immediately entering another trade to
         "make the money back." The new trade is driven by emotion,
         not analysis. It almost always loses too.
         Rule: After any loss, take a 15-minute break. Minimum.

      2. FOMO — Fear of Missing Out
         Entering a trade because price has already moved strongly
         and you don't want to miss the rest of the move.
         You are entering late, into an extended move, with a poor
         entry, a wide SL, and a small remaining target.
         Rule: If you missed the setup, you missed it. The market
               will give you another opportunity.

      3. EARLY EXIT
         Closing a winning trade before it reaches Take Profit because
         you are afraid it will turn around. This systematically
         destroys your R/R ratio. A 1:2 R/R trade exited at 1:0.8 is
         a losing strategy long-term even if the trade was "profitable."
         Rule: If your analysis was correct for the entry, trust it
               for the exit. Move your SL to breakeven instead.

      4. STOP WIDENING
         Moving your Stop Loss further away when price approaches it,
         hoping the trade will recover.
         Rule: Never move a Stop Loss against your position.
               You can move it in your favour (trailing). Never away.

      5. OVERTRADING
         Taking too many trades in a single session. Each trade taken
         without a clear setup reduces your overall edge. Decision
         fatigue sets in. Quality drops.
         Rule: Set a maximum trade limit per session. Two or three
               quality setups are better than ten mediocre ones.

      6. TILT PATTERN
         A cascade of emotional decisions following a losing period.
         Increasing position sizes, abandoning your strategy, trading
         instruments you don't understand. The spiral before the
         account blowup.
         Rule: If you catch yourself in tilt, close the platform.
               Return tomorrow. The market will always be there.

  4.6 Building a Trading Plan
      The most successful traders are boring. They do the same thing,
      the same way, every session. Their plan answers:
        1. Which instruments will I trade?
        2. Which timeframe is my primary analysis chart?
        3. Which timeframe is my entry chart?
        4. What is my maximum risk per trade?
        5. What is my maximum daily loss before I stop?
        6. What constitutes a valid entry signal for me?
        7. What constitutes a valid exit signal (other than SL/TP)?
        8. What sessions will I trade?

      Without a plan, you are gambling. With a plan, you are testing
      a systematic approach that can be refined over time.

  4.7 Journalling — The Professional Habit
      Every professional trader keeps a trading journal. Not to track
      wins and losses, but to identify patterns in their own behaviour.

      After every trade, record:
        Entry price, direction, instrument, session
        Why you entered (setup description)
        SL and TP levels and reasoning
        Result (profit/loss in R, not dollars — dollars mislead)
        Emotion before entry (calm / excited / anxious / frustrated)
        What you would do differently

      After 50 trades, patterns emerge. You will see which setups
      work for you, which sessions you trade best, and which emotional
      states lead to poor decisions. The journal is your feedback loop.

      Wingman's Sim Trader auto-populates the journal. Your job is to
      add the emotional state and post-trade reflection.

  5.1 Fibonacci Retracements and Extensions
      Based on the Fibonacci sequence (each number is the sum of the
      previous two: 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21...).
      The key ratios are: 23.6%, 38.2%, 50%, 61.8%, 78.6%, 100%

      RETRACEMENTS: Used to find potential reversal areas within
      a move. When price makes a strong move (swing low to swing high),
      it often retraces to a Fibonacci level before continuing.

      How to draw:
        In an uptrend: Click from the swing LOW to the swing HIGH.
          The Fibonacci levels draw between those two points.
          Price often retraces to the 38.2%, 50%, or 61.8% level
          before continuing upward.
        In a downtrend: Click from the swing HIGH to the swing LOW.

      The 61.8% level (the "Golden Ratio") is the most powerful.
      When the 61.8% level coincides with a key S/R zone, it is a
      very high-confidence reversal area.

      EXTENSIONS: Project where price may go AFTER the retracement.
      Common targets: 127.2%, 161.8%, 261.8% of the initial move.
      These are used to set Take Profit targets.

  5.2 Trendlines and Channels
      A trendline connects successive swing lows (in an uptrend)
      or successive swing highs (in a downtrend).

      Rules for a valid trendline:
        - Minimum two touch points to draw it
        - Three touch points makes it significant
        - Four or more touch points makes it very significant

      CHANNELS: Draw a parallel line to the trendline on the other
      side. Price often oscillates between the two lines.
        Ascending channel: Both lines sloping up. Bullish.
        Descending channel: Both lines sloping down. Bearish.
        Horizontal channel: Both lines flat. Ranging market.

      Trendline BREAKS are among the most reliable signals:
      When price closes clearly through a major trendline with
      momentum and high volume (or tick volume in forex), the
      structure has changed.

  5.3 Chart Patterns
      Patterns that repeat across all markets and timeframes because
      human psychology is consistent. These are high-probability setups
      when combined with confluence.

      CONTINUATION PATTERNS (trend continues after):
        Flag:        A sharp move (the pole) followed by a tight
                     consolidation (the flag). Entry on the break
                     of the flag in the direction of the pole.
        Pennant:     Similar to a flag but the consolidation forms
                     a symmetrical triangle shape.
        Wedge:       Price moves in converging lines in the same
                     direction. A falling wedge in a downtrend often
                     resolves bullishly. A rising wedge in an uptrend
                     often resolves bearishly.

      REVERSAL PATTERNS (trend changes after):
        Head and Shoulders (H&S):
          Three peaks: left shoulder, higher head, right shoulder.
          A neckline connects the two troughs between the peaks.
          Break of the neckline = bearish reversal signal.
          Target: measure the height of the head above the neckline
          and project it downward from the break.

        Inverse H&S: Same pattern upside down. Bullish reversal.

        Double Top: Price reaches a high, pulls back, rallies to
          approximately the same high, fails. Break of the
          pullback low (the "neck") is the sell signal.

        Double Bottom: Inverse. Two similar lows. Break of the
          pullback high is the buy signal.

        Cup and Handle: A rounded bottom (the cup) followed by a
          small consolidation (the handle). Breakout above the
          rim of the cup is a strong bullish signal.

  5.4 Market Context: The Tools Beyond Price
      Individual candles and indicators tell you about price.
      Broader context tells you about the forces driving price.

      ECONOMIC CALENDAR:
        Scheduled news events that move markets. Categories:
        High impact (red):  Central bank decisions, NFP, CPI, GDP.
                            Can move a pair 50-200+ pips in minutes.
        Medium impact (orange): PMI, retail sales, employment data.
        Low impact (yellow): Minor data releases. Usually ignored.

        Professional rule: Do not hold trades through high-impact news
        unless you understand exactly what the release means and have
        widened your SL to account for the spike. Most professionals
        either close positions before news or sit out.

      COMMITMENT OF TRADERS (COT) REPORT:
        Published weekly by the CFTC (Commodity Futures Trading Commission).
        Shows the net positions of three groups:
          Commercials: Hedgers (producers, manufacturers). They often
                       hold opposite positions to the market — when they
                       are heavily short, they are hedging, not speculating.
          Non-commercials (Large Specs): Hedge funds and institutional
                       traders. These are the "smart money." When they
                       are heavily long, the trend is likely to continue.
          Retail (Small Specs): Often wrong at extremes.

        How to use it:
          Look for extremes in non-commercial positioning.
          When large specs are at a multi-year high in long positions,
          the trend they are riding is likely well-established.
          When they begin cutting positions, watch for a reversal.
          Do not use COT as a short-term entry signal — it is a
          3-day delayed report and a context indicator, not a trigger.

      RETAIL SENTIMENT:
        Shows what percentage of retail traders are long vs short.
        Source: Brokers like OANDA report their client book.
        The contrarian principle: retail traders are often wrong
        at extremes. If 80% of retail traders are long on a pair,
        consider whether the smart money is on the other side.
        Used as context, not as a trigger.

      FEAR AND GREED INDEX:
        Composite index for crypto/risk sentiment.
        Extreme Fear: Markets have sold off heavily. Potential buying
                      opportunity for patient traders.
        Extreme Greed: Markets are euphoric. Risk of a correction.
        Used as a background filter, not a precise entry signal.

  5.5 Expert Advisors (EAs) and Algorithmic Trading
      An Expert Advisor is an automated trading program that runs on
      a platform (typically MetaTrader 4 or 5) and executes trades
      automatically based on programmed rules.

      What EAs can do:
        - Execute entries and exits without human intervention
        - Manage multiple trades simultaneously
        - Never experience fear, greed, or fatigue
        - Backtest strategies on historical data

      What EAs cannot do:
        - Adapt to changing market regimes without being reprogrammed
        - Understand context (news, sentiment, structural change)
        - Know when the market has fundamentally changed
        - Replace human judgment in complex situations

      Types of EAs:
        TREND-FOLLOWING: Buy when MA crosses, sell on opposite cross.
                         Works well in trending markets. Loses in ranges.
        SCALPING:        Very short-term trades, small targets.
                         Sensitive to spread and slippage.
        GRID:            Places trades at regular price intervals.
                         Extremely dangerous in trending markets.
        MARTINGALE:      Doubles position size after every loss.
                         Guaranteed to blow the account eventually.
                         Avoid completely.
        MEAN REVERSION:  Bets price will return to an average.
                         Works in ranges. Catastrophic in trends.

      The Wingman Autotrader Intelligence Engine uses rule-based AI
      to observe market conditions — it is conceptually similar to
      an EA but uses a large language model for pattern recognition
      rather than fixed mathematical rules.

      EA SCAMS: The retail trading world is full of EAs sold for
      $200-$2,000 with "guaranteed" returns. They nearly all fail.
      Any EA claiming consistent high returns without drawdown is
      fraudulent. Real professional algorithmic systems are not
      sold to retail traders on websites.

  5.6 Order Types Beyond Market Orders
      Market Order: Execute immediately at current price. Subject to
                    slippage during fast markets.

      Limit Order:  Execute only at a specific price or better.
                    BUY LIMIT: Execute at or below specified price.
                    SELL LIMIT: Execute at or above specified price.
                    Used to enter at a specific level without watching
                    the screen. Risk: price may never reach your level.

      Stop Order:   Execute when price reaches a specified level.
                    BUY STOP: Execute when price rises to specified level
                               (used to trade breakouts upward).
                    SELL STOP: Execute when price falls to specified level
                                (used to trade breakouts downward).

      Stop-Limit:   A stop order that becomes a limit order once triggered.
                    Protects against excessive slippage on breakouts.

      Trailing Stop: A stop loss that moves with the price as the trade
                     profits. If price rises 50 pips and your trailing
                     stop is 20 pips, your stop rises with it, locking in
                     30 pips of profit while leaving room for more.

  6.1 What Makes a Strategy a Strategy
      A strategy is a repeatable, rule-based approach to entering and
      exiting the market. It must be specific enough that two different
      traders following it would take the same trade.

      "I buy when price looks good" is not a strategy.
      "I buy when price breaks above a 20-period high on the 4H chart,
       with RSI above 50, and the 50 EMA pointing up, risking 1% of
       my account with a 1:2 R/R" is a strategy.

      A strategy has six components:
        1. Market/instrument (what you trade)
        2. Timeframe (where you analyse and enter)
        3. Entry criteria (exactly what triggers entry)
        4. Stop Loss rule (exactly how you calculate it)
        5. Take Profit rule (exactly how you calculate it)
        6. Trade management (what you do while in the trade)

  6.2 The Main Strategy Categories

      TREND FOLLOWING:
        Buy in uptrends, sell in downtrends.
        Enter on pullbacks, not at peaks.
        Hold until the trend structure breaks.
        Best instruments: Forex majors, Gold, indices.
        Best timeframes: 4H, Daily.
        Tools: EMAs, MACD, Fibonacci retracements for entries.
        Risk: Choppy, ranging markets destroy trend systems.

      BREAKOUT TRADING:
        Enter when price breaks through a key level with momentum.
        Anticipate the level, wait for the close, enter on confirmation.
        Set SL just below the broken level (now acting as support).
        Target: projection of the range that contained price.
        Risk: False breakouts. Price breaks the level, looks like
        a setup, then reverses back. Always wait for candle close.

      MEAN REVERSION (Fade Trading):
        Bet that price will return to average after an extreme move.
        Buy when price is far below a moving average in a range.
        Sell when price is far above a moving average in a range.
        Tools: Bollinger Bands, RSI overbought/oversold, stochastic.
        Risk: In a trending market, "extreme" can become "more extreme."
        This strategy has the highest failure rate for beginners
        who apply it without checking the broader trend context.

      SCALPING:
        Very short-term trades. Seconds to minutes.
        Target: 5–15 pips / points.
        Stop Loss: 5–10 pips / points.
        Requires: Fast execution, low spreads, high concentration.
        Time commitment: Requires full attention during the session.
        Best in: London/NY overlap (highest liquidity, tightest spreads).
        Risk: Spread as a percentage of target is very high. If you
        target 5 pips and the spread is 1.5 pips, you immediately start
        30% in the negative on every trade.

      SWING TRADING:
        Holds trades for hours to days. Catches "swings" in price.
        Less time-intensive than scalping. Works around a full-time job.
        Best for: Retail traders who cannot watch screens all day.
        Tools: 4H and Daily chart analysis, Fibonacci, S/R, trendlines.
        Risk: Holds through news events and weekend gaps.

      POSITION TRADING:
        Holds trades for weeks to months. Based on fundamental analysis
        and major trend direction.
        Best for: Highly capitalised accounts.
        Least suitable for: Small accounts where drawdown tolerance is low.

  6.3 The ICT / Smart Money Concepts Framework
      Increasingly popular methodology among retail traders.
      Based on the idea that institutional traders (banks, funds)
      create liquidity by manipulating retail stop levels.

      Key concepts:
        ORDER BLOCKS: Areas where institutional orders were placed.
          Often appear as the last bullish candle before a strong
          bearish move, or the last bearish candle before a strong
          bullish move. Price often returns to these zones.

        FAIR VALUE GAPS (FVG / Imbalance):
          When price moves so fast that a candle leaves a "gap" in
          the market (the high of the candle before it does not touch
          the low of the candle after it). Price tends to return to
          fill this gap before continuing.

        LIQUIDITY SWEEPS:
          Price moves just beyond a previous high or low (where stops
          are clustered), then reverses. The sweep "collected" the
          stop orders to fill institutional positions.

        DISPLACEMENT:
          A strong, impulsive move away from a level. Usually indicates
          institutional participation.

      This framework is powerful but complex. It requires significant
      screen time and practice to apply reliably. Wingman Academy uses
      elements of it in Stage 6 and 7.

  6.4 Backtesting — Testing Your Strategy on History
      Before trading any strategy live (even in the Sim Trader with
      real-time data), test it on historical data first.

      Manual backtesting process:
        1. Open a chart on the timeframe you intend to trade
        2. Cover the right side of the chart (the future)
        3. Reveal one candle at a time
        4. Apply your strategy rules exactly
        5. Record every trade you would have taken
        6. After 50–100 trades, calculate:
             Win rate, Average R, Maximum drawdown,
             Longest losing streak, Profit factor

      What backtesting tells you:
        - Whether the strategy has positive expectancy
        - How it performs in trending vs ranging conditions
        - Your maximum drawdown to set realistic expectations

      What backtesting does not tell you:
        - Whether it will work in the future
        - How you will emotionally handle the losing streaks
        - The psychological challenge of live (or real-time sim) trading

      Forward testing (paper trading in real time) bridges the gap.
      That is what the Wingman Sim Trader is for.

  6.5 Building Your Personal Trading Plan
      By Stage 6, students have the knowledge to build a proper plan.
      This is the deliverable for Stage 6.

      The plan must cover:
        Instruments:       List the 1-3 instruments you will specialise in
        Sessions:          Which session(s) you will trade
        Primary timeframe: Your analysis chart
        Entry timeframe:   Your execution chart (usually 1–2 steps lower)
        Entry criteria:    Specific rules. What must be true to enter?
        Invalid setups:    What conditions make you NOT enter?
        Stop Loss method:  ATR-based? Behind structure? Fixed?
        TP method:         Fixed target? Fibonacci extension? Trailing?
        Max risk per trade: As % of account
        Daily stop:        Maximum loss before you stop for the day
        Weekly review:     When you will review your journal

STAGE 7 — THE PROFESSIONAL STANDARD
Subtitle: "Consistency is the only proof of competence."
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  LEARN — LESSON CONTENT
  ───────────────────────

  7.1 What Professional Traders Actually Do
      Professional traders at funds and banks do not trade
      on instinct. They follow process, risk frameworks, and
      structured evaluation. They are judged not on individual
      trades but on risk-adjusted returns over time.

      The metrics that matter professionally:
        PROFIT FACTOR: Gross profit ÷ Gross loss.
                        Below 1.0 = losing system.
                        1.0–1.5 = marginal.
                        1.5–2.0 = decent.
                        Above 2.0 = strong.
        SHARPE RATIO:   Return relative to volatility/risk.
                        Higher is better. Above 1.0 is acceptable.
        MAX DRAWDOWN:   Maximum peak-to-trough decline.
                        Professional funds rarely tolerate above 20%.
        WIN RATE:       Less important than profit factor, but matters
                        for psychological sustainability. Below 30%
                        is very hard to maintain psychologically even if
                        mathematically positive.
        AVERAGE R:      Average return in units of risk.
                        Positive average R + consistent application = profit.

  7.2 Prop Firm Trading and Funded Accounts
      Proprietary trading firms give traders access to institutional
      capital in exchange for a share of the profits.
      Popular firms: FTMO, MyForexFunds, The Funded Trader.

      The evaluation process (a "challenge"):
        Phase 1: Hit a profit target (e.g. 10%) without exceeding
                 drawdown limits (e.g. 5% daily, 10% overall)
        Phase 2: Repeat with a smaller target to confirm consistency
        Funded: Trade the firm's money. Keep 70-90% of profits.

      The Wingman Academy Stage 7 challenge is designed to simulate
      a prop firm evaluation — demonstrating that you can trade
      consistently within strict risk parameters.

      Rules that prop firms universally apply:
        - Maximum daily loss (usually 4-5% of account)
        - Maximum overall drawdown (usually 8-12%)
        - Minimum trading days (usually 4+)
        - No trading during major news events (some firms)
        - Consistent strategy (no random switching)

  7.3 Institutional Market Mechanics
      How markets actually move at the macro level:

      SMART MONEY CYCLE:
        1. Accumulation: Institutions quietly build positions.
           Price ranges. Retail traders are bored and leave.
        2. Markup: Institutions begin to drive price in their direction.
           Trend begins. Retail traders notice and start entering.
        3. Distribution: Institutions quietly exit positions into the
           retail buying/selling frenzy at the top/bottom.
        4. Markdown/Re-accumulation: Price reverses. Retail traders
           are now holding losing positions.

      Understanding this cycle explains why trends end when "everyone"
      is talking about them, and why the best entries often feel
      uncomfortable.

  7.4 Fundamental Analysis — The Macro Layer
      Technical analysis describes what price is doing.
      Fundamental analysis explains WHY it might continue or reverse.

      KEY FUNDAMENTALS FOR FOREX:
        Interest rates: The most powerful driver of currency strength.
          Higher rates attract foreign capital. The currency strengthens.
          Central banks (Fed, ECB, BoE, BoJ) set rates.
          Follow: FOMC meetings, ECB meetings, BoE meetings.

        Inflation (CPI): Central banks raise rates to fight inflation.
          Rising CPI often leads to rate hike expectations.
          Rate hike expectations strengthen the currency.

        Employment (NFP, jobs data): Strong employment → strong economy
          → potential rate rises → stronger currency.
          NFP (US Non-Farm Payrolls) is the most market-moving
          monthly data point in forex.

        GDP: Measures economic growth. Strong growth = stronger currency.
             Recession fears = weaker currency.

        Geopolitics: Wars, elections, trade disputes all move markets.
             Cannot be predicted. Can only be managed with risk controls.

      The practical approach for retail traders:
        Know the direction of interest rate expectations.
        Trade currencies where rate expectations are diverging
        (one central bank hiking, another on hold or cutting).
        Use fundamental context as a background filter.
        Use technical analysis for precise entries and exits.

  7.5 The Final Review: Self-Assessment Framework
      Before claiming professional readiness, honestly answer:

        □ Can I describe my strategy in under 60 seconds?
        □ Do I follow my trading plan on 90%+ of trades?
        □ Has my behaviour score improved since Stage 1?
        □ Have I had zero stop widening instances in my last 20 trades?
        □ Have I had zero revenge trades in my last 20 trades?
        □ Do I understand why I take every trade I take?
        □ Do I have a daily and weekly review habit?
        □ Could I pass a prop firm evaluation on my current performance?

      If the answer to any of these is "no," Stage 7 is not about
      learning more content. It is about doing the work to make
      the answer "yes."
